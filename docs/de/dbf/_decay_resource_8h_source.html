95  T1/2 - half-life of the original nuclide (seconds).
96 
97  b)
98  RTYP Mode of decay of the nuclide in its LIS state.
99  Decay modes are deﬁned as follows:
100  RTYP Decay Mode
101  0. γ γ-ray (not used in MT457)
102  1. β Beta decay
103  2. e.c., β+ Electron capture and/or positron emission
104  3. IT Isomeric transition (will in general be present only when the
105  state being considered is an isomeric state)
106  4. α Alpha decay
107  5. n Neutron emission (not delayed neutron decay, see below)
108  6. SF Spontaneous ﬁssion
109  7. p Proton emission
110  10. Unknown origin
111 
112  Multiple particle decay is also allowed using any combination of the above
113  RTYP variables as illustrated in the following examples:
114  RTYP Decay Mode
115  1.5 β−,n Beta decay followed by neutron emission (delayed neutron decay)
116  1.4 β−,α Beta decay followed by alpha emission (e.g. 16N decay)
117  2.4 β+,α Positron decay followed by alpha emission.
118 
119  RFS Isomeric state ﬂag for daughter nuclide.
120  RFS=0.0, ground state;
121  RFS=1.0, ﬁrst isomeric state, etc.
122 
123  Q Total decay energy (eV) available in the corresponding decay process. This
124  is not necessarily the same as the maximum energy of the emitted radiation.
125  In the case of an isomeric transition Q will be the energy of the isomeric
126  state. For both β+ and β−, Q equals the energy corresponding to the mass
127  difference between the initial and ﬁnal atoms).
128 
129  BR Fraction of the decay of the nuclide in its LIS state which proceeds by
130  the

132  the daughter nucleus are excited then BR=1.0 for β− decay.
